# Purpose of Cash Flow Statements

## Why do we need a Cash Flow Statement?

The Statement of Comprehensive Income (Income Statement) shows **Profit**, which includes non-cash items (like credit sales, depreciation, and bad debts).
The **Cash Flow Statement** shows the actual **movement of cash** in and out of the business.

**Key Purpose:**

1. To show where the company got its money (Inflows).
2. To show how the company spent its money (Outflows).
3. To show the net change in *Cash and Cash Equivalents*.

---

## Cash vs Profit

It is possible for a company to make a huge profit but have no cash (e.g., if all sales are on credit and debtors haven't paid).

- **Profit**: Calculated on the *Accrual Basis* (income earned, expenses incurred).
- **Cash Flow**: Calculated on the *Cash Basis* (money actually received or paid).

---

## The Three Categories

The Cash Flow Statement is divided into three main sections:

### 1. Cash Effects of Operating Activities

Day-to-day business operations.

- **Inflows**: Cash from customers, Interest received, Dividends received.
- **Outflows**: Payments to suppliers, employees, interest paid, tax paid, dividends paid.

### 2. Cash Effects of Investing Activities

Buying and selling long-term assets.

- **Inflows**: Selling fixed assets, decreasing fixed deposits.
- **Outflows**: Buying fixed assets, increasing fixed deposits.

### 3. Cash Effects of Financing Activities

Funding the business (Owners and Lenders).

- **Inflows**: Issuing shares, taking out a loan.
- **Outflows**: Buying back shares, repaying a loan.

---

## Exam Tips

- **Bank Overdraft**: In the Cash Flow Statement, a bank overdraft is treated as a negative cash equivalent.
- **Signage**: Always indicate outflows with brackets `( )`.
- **Logic Check**: If you buy a vehicle, cash goes *out*. If you sell a vehicle, cash comes *in*.
